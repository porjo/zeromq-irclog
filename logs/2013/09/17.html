[00:09]michelp russkey, the pump and sink, and workers, can all often be on the same machine. For some languages like Python this is a great way to take advantage of machines with multiple CPUs
[00:09]russkey michelp: I understand that part. I mean, why not combine the pump and sink into a single thread? why do most of the examples I've come across seem to have them in different threads or different processes or different machines? Am I just missing the examples that show otherwise?
[00:09]taotetek russkey: I suppose it depends on how many messages a second you're trying to deal with
[00:09]taotetek russkey: and how many mbit/s
[00:09]taotetek russkey: if you have a problem you're trying to parallelize where the workers will be working on one unit of work for awhile, probably couldn't hurt to have pump and sink in same proc
[00:09]russkey that is the situation, indeed. more than that, the work is sequential, so all of the workers need to be done and some integration needs to happen at the sink before the next calculation can start
[00:09]russkey this is an agent based simulation
[00:09]taotetek russkey: then from my own experience I'd say you'd be fine doing it that way
[00:09]taotetek russkey: if you end up bottlenecking you can always move the sink to a separate process (or thread) later
[00:09]russkey well that is encouraging, then. i was worried i was missing some sort of deep and important law
[00:09]taotetek russkey: nope! I think most of the examples have sink separate just assuming that you might not want the work the sink is doing interfering with the ventilator sending messages
[00:09]taotetek russkey: but if you have a case where your bottleneck will be the work your workers are doing and not so much the messaging itself, there's no other concerns
[00:09]russkey thanks, taotetek!
[00:09]taotetek no problem!
